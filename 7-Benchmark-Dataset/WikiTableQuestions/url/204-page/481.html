The 2014 New York gubernatorial election will take place on November 4, 2014, to elect the Governor of New York. Incumbent Democratic Governor Andrew Cuomo is eligible to run for re-election to a second term in office. Though he has not formally announced his intentions, he has renamed his campaign committee "Andrew Cuomo 2014" and has distributed campaign items.[1]

Background

Democrat Andrew Cuomo, the Attorney General of New York, was elected Governor in 2010, defeating Republican businessman Carl Paladino in a landslide - 63% to 33%. He succeeded retiring Democratic Governor David Paterson.

Republicans do not believe Cuomo is vulnerable, calling him a "shoo-in for re-election",[2][3] citing his high popularity and large campaign warchest, which stood at $33 million in January 2014. By contrast, Cuomo spent $28 million in the entire 2010 campaign.[4] It is believed that the Republicans will nominate someone who is not up for re-election in 2014 and so doesn't have to give up their office to run, who would use the campaign to raise their profile for a future run at statewide office.[5]

Progressive minor parties see an opportunity to make headway in the state due to Cuomo's relatively conservative stances on taxes and spending. A poll commissioned by Bill Samuels indicated that even an unknown left-wing third-party challenger could garner as much as 13 percent of the vote without threatening Cuomo's chances of winning re-election; since ballot order in the state is determined by gubernatorial votes, this would easily propel whatever party ran that candidate past the Conservative Party and assure the party the third line on the ballot through 2018.[6]

Major third parties

Besides the Democratic and Republican parties, the Conservative, Green, Independence and Working Families parties are qualified New York parties. These parties have automatic ballot access.

Conservative

Although the Conservative Party traditionally cross-endorses Republicans in most races, it has occasionally broken rank and nominated its own candidates (in gubernatorial races, this most recently happened in 1990 with Herb London).

Conservative Party chairman Michael R. Long endorsed Rob Astorino in February 2014.[7] Carl Paladino, currently a Buffalo Public Schools Board of Education member and the Republican nominee for Governor in 2010, had originally stated he would seek the Conservative Party line if the Republicans nominate Rob Astorino,[21] but by March 2014 had withdrawn from any potential race and has stated he would (lukewarmly) support Astorino if Donald Trump were not to run.[19]

Green

In contrast to the other qualified parties, the Green Party of New York traditionally endorses its own candidates.

Candidates

Declared
  • Howie Hawkins, activist, perennial candidate and Green Party nominee for Governor in 2010[22]

Independence

The Independence Party of New York, which traditionally cross-endorses the candidate most likely to get them the most votes, is most likely to nominate incumbent Governor Andrew Cuomo as it did in 2010. Republican Rob Astorino has already refused the line, and several members of the Democratic Party have called on Cuomo to do the same.[23]

Working Families

The Working Families Party traditionally cross-endorses Democrats but has expressed reservations over endorsing incumbent Governor Andrew Cuomo as they did in 2010.[24]

Candidates

Potential
  • Dan Cantor, Executive Director of the Working Families Party
  • Andrew Cuomo, incumbent Governor
  • Bill Samuels, activist[23]

Minor third parties

Any candidate not among the six qualified New York parties (Democratic, Republican, Conservative, Green, Independence and Working Families) must petition their way onto the ballot; they do not face primary elections.

Liberal

With an eye to regaining ballot access, the Liberal Party of New York is openly considering endorsing Governor Cuomo in 2014.[25][26]
